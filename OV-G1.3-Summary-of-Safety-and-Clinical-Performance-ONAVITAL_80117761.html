1. Introduction & Scope

This Summary of Safety and Clinical Performance (SSCP) is intended to provide public access to an updated summary of the main aspects of the safety and clinical performance of the device. The information presented below is intended for patients or lay persons. A more extensive summary of its safety and clinical performance prepared for healthcare professionals is found in clinical evaluation report.

The SSCP is not intended to give general advice on the treatment of a medical condition. Please contact your healthcare professional in case you have questions about your medical condition or about the use of the device in your situation. This SSCP is not intended to replace an Implant card or the Instructions For Use to provide information on the safe use of the device.

1. Device identification and general information

Device trade name: ONAVITAL

Basic UDI-DI 8437026494001

Year when the device was first CE-marked: 2024*

ONAVITAL TM is a wearable physical sensor bracelet worn by the patient/user including sensors and calibration algorithms for vital signs acquisition and adapters for data digitalization. It provides provides accurate and precise measurements of Pulse Rate (PR), Oxygen Saturation (SpO2), Systolic and Diastolic Arterial Blood Pressure (ABP) and Skin-surface Temperature (ST) through a wearable physical bracelet.

2. Intended use of the device

ONAVITAL by Onalabs Inno-hub SL is remote monitoring device for spot check collection of accurate measurements of Pulse Rate (PR), Oxygen Saturation (SpO2), Arterial Blood Pressure (ABP) and Skin Temperature (STEMP). ONAVITAL is intended for spot-checking and tracking vital signs of adult patients in general and cardiorespiratory patients in particular.

Indications and intended patient groups

  1. Adult patients in general and cardiorespiratory patients in particular. Pulse oximeters can be used on most pediatrics to adults, however ONAVITAL has been designed exclusively for Adult users.

  2. Patients that are not considered to be in a critical condition.

ONAVITAL TM is intended to be used by patients that are not considered to be in a critical condition.

ONAVITAL TM can be used on any location where there is internet connectivity whether they are private residences or other environments such as nursing homes, primary care centres.

ONAVITAL provides:

  • Remote spot-check measurement of patients' vital signs.

  • Remote measurement of oxygen saturation with accuracy (reported as Accuracy Root Mean Square or Arms) within 4% of commercial medical grade pulsi oximeters arterial blood gas values.

  • Remote measurement of pulse rate with accuracy (reported as Mean Absolute Error) within 3BPM of commercial medical grade pulsi oximeters pulse rate values.

  • Remote measurement of systolic and diastolic arterial blood pressure (reported as Standard Deviation) within 8 mmHg of commercial medical grade sphygmomanometer values.

  • Remote measurement of skin-surface temperature (reported as SD) within the 1ºC of commercial medical grade thermometers values.

Contraindications

  • People without the skills and competencies to operate the digital solution.

  • Pulse oximeters have limitations and a risk of inaccuracy under certain circumstances. In many cases, the level of inaccuracy may be small and not clinically meaningful; however, there is a risk that an inaccurate measurement may result in unrecognized low oxygen saturation levels.

  • The SpO2 reading should always be considered an estimate of oxygen saturation. For example, if a pulse oximeter reads 90%, then the true oxygen saturation in the blood is generally between 86-94%. Pulse oximeter accuracy is highest at saturations of 90-100%, intermediate at 80-90%, and lowest below 80%.

  • Due to accuracy limitations at the individual level, SpO2 provides more utility for trends over time instead of absolute thresholds.

  • Many patient factors may also affect the accuracy of the measurement. The most current scientific evidence shows that there are some accuracy differences in pulse oximeters between dark and light skin pigmentation; this difference is typically small at saturations above 80% and greater when saturations are less than 80%.

  • Wireless pulse oximeters do not have unlimited range. To work effectively the wireless oximeter must be in Bluetooth range of the device they are communicating with.

  • Do not use with neonatal patients.

  • If the device is mechanically damaged it must not be used and must be disposed.

  • The device have been designed and evaluated to be used in adults, do not use on paediatrics.

  • Do not use with patients with significant deformity, swelling, irritation, degenerative changes or edema of
    the wrist.

  • Do not use the wrist monitor on patients with localized infection, ulceration or skin lesions involving the wrist.

  • Do not use the wrist monitor on patients that have restricted blood flow e.g. tourniquet, pressure cuff or IV line.

  • Do not use with patients with tremors or convulsions.

  • Do not use with patients with peripheral vascular disease affecting the hands.

  • Do not use on an area with a tattoo.

  • DO NOT use in MRI or a CT environment.

4. Accessories

The following accessories are provided:

  • Cloud based platform used to collect and throughput data from the wearable bracelet to the mobile application for visualisation. Data from the wearable is securely transmitted via an associated mobile phone to a cloud-based platform for storage and visualisation. Data securely held on the platform is then displayed by the mobile application to an individual user, or can be collated into summary dashboards or shared via APIs. The platform functions as a medical device data system and is used to manage authentication and associate device data with registered individuals.

  • IOS and Android apps for visualisation. The application enables users to visualise the data captured by the wearable component.

Cloud-based platform:

The cloud based platform is used to manage a registry of wearable bracelets, associate bracelets with individuals, provide authentication to individuals accessing the ONAVITAL application, and act as a data repository. Data from wearable bracelets is associated with individuals on the cloud-based platform and the platform has functionality to visualise data and make available data reports through APIs or cloud-based dashboard.

The ONAVITAL device does not include animal or human tissue or blood products or derivatives thereof or products that would be medicinal substances or use prohibited substances as defined in the Essential Requirements nor constitute a machine or personal protection equipment. There are no sterility requirements for the device.

Mobile Application (IOs and Android)

The mobile application which runs on IOS and Android operating systems enables 1. data visualisation and 2. data transfer using bluetooth low-energy (BLE). Data visualisation occurs through the application, and the app includes adapters that allow data from the wearable bracelet to be securely transferred to a cloud-based platform and external services using HTTP REST or MQTT.

    • Excessive motion

    • Intravascular dyes

  • The device has no audible alarms and is intended for periodic spot checking.

  • Real-world accuracy may differ from accuracy in the lab setting. While reported accuracy is an average of all patients in the test sample, there are individual variations among patients. The SpO2 reading should always be considered an estimate of oxygen saturation. For example, if a pulse oximeter reads 90%, then the true oxygen saturation in the blood is generally between 86-94%. 6. Summary of clinical evaluation and post-market clinical follow-up

6.1 Clinical background of the device

ONAVITAL has been developed with clinical investigation procedures done on two of the most prominent healthcare centers in Barcelona, Hospital Universitari Germans Trias i Pujol and Hospital Universitari de Bellvitge, through the different development stages to assess the safety and accuracy of the different prototypes on each one of the product lifecycle stages, as well as helping the development providing clinical resources during calibration studies.

The planned clinical investigation have been completed with appropriate safety standards and approval from the ethics committee.

The clinical investigation included different evaluation stages, where it is studied the ability of ONAVITAL to achieve its intended purpose of spot check collection of accurate measurements of Pulse Rate (PR), Oxygen Saturation (SpO2), Arterial Blood Pressure (ABP) and Skin-surface Temperature (STEMP) in adult patients in general and cardiorespiratory patients in particular.

In these studies the main goals were to evaluate if ONAVITAL achieve their intended performances during normal conditions of use on adult subjects.

Also, a thorough literature review of relevant scientific literature related to wearable non-invasive vital signs monitoring devices, and a compilation of published studies, case reports, and other relevant information has been done. In addition, a comparative analysis, highlighting the advantages, disadvantages and similarities of the new device in comparison to other similar MDR certified devices, have been done.

5.2 The clinical evidence for the CE-marking

The clinical evidence suggest that ONAVITAL yields equivalent vital sign values to those obtained via multiparametric monitors and/or fingertip-based PPG monitors and cuff-based BP monitors. 6. Possible diagnostic or therapeutic alternatives

When considering alternative treatments, it is recommended to contact your healthcare professional who can take into account your individual situation. While ONAVITAL can be used for non-invasive monitoring, several alternative approaches exist for assessing more specific conditions. Some common therapeutic alternatives include:

  1. Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) Analysis:

Description: Obtaining a sample of arterial blood allows for direct measurement of oxygen and carbon dioxide levels, as well as pH and other blood gas parameters.
Use Cases: Often employed in critical care settings or situations where precise oxygenation and acid-base status are crucial.

  1. Capnography:

Description: Measures the concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) in exhaled breath, providing insights into respiratory function and ventilation.
Use Cases: Useful in monitoring patients under anesthesia, during mechanical ventilation, and in emergency situations.

  1. Lung Auscultation:

Description: Listening to breath sounds using a stethoscope to assess lung function, detect abnormalities, and infer potential respiratory issues.
Use Cases: Often employed in routine physical examinations and in identifying conditions like pneumonia or bronchitis.

  1. Pulse Palpation and Auscultation:

Description: Assessing blood pressure indirectly by feeling peripheral pulses or listening to Korotkoff sounds using a stethoscope.
Use Cases: Common in routine clinical examinations, particularly when immediate numerical values may not be required.

  1. Invasive Arterial Blood Pressure (IBP) Monitoring:

Description: Directly measuring blood pressure by inserting a catheter into an artery, typically the radial or femoral artery.
Use Cases: Common in critical care settings, surgery, or situations where continuous and precise blood pressure monitoring is essential.

  1. Central Venous Pressure (CVP) Monitoring:

Description: Measuring the pressure in the central venous circulation, typically through a catheter placed in a large vein, such as the superior vena cava.
Use Cases: Used to assess fluid status and guide fluid administration, especially in critically ill patients.

  1. Manual Sphygmomanometer with Doppler:

Description: Combining a traditional blood pressure cuff with a Doppler ultrasound device to assess blood pressure in patients with challenging anatomy or limited access.
Use Cases: Common in vascular assessments and in situations where standard cuff-based measurements are difficult.

These therapeutic alternatives cater to diverse clinical scenarios, providing healthcare professionals with a range of tools to assess and manage patients' vital signs status beyond PPG non-invasive technology. The choice of method depends on the specific clinical context, patient condition, and monitoring requirements, where the expert clinicians can choose from.
